Some reefs are ecosystems on a scale that rewrites what "underwater" can mean —
visible from orbit, holding a quarter of marine life, or dropping into the
abyss. These are the planet's reef icons.

## Great Barrier Reef, Australia

The largest living structure on Earth — 2,300 km of coral visible from space,
the benchmark every other reef is measured against.

## Raja Ampat, Indonesia

The global epicentre of marine biodiversity in the Coral Triangle — more reef
fish and coral species than anywhere known.

## Tubbataha Reefs, Philippines

A remote, fiercely protected atoll complex, one of the most pristine reef
systems left.

## Belize Barrier Reef & the Great Blue Hole

The largest reef in the Northern Hemisphere, punctuated by a perfect submarine
sinkhole that became a diving legend.

## Palau

A maze of reefs, walls, and Jellyfish Lake — a marine sanctuary nation that
pioneered reef protection.

## Bonaire

A "shore-diving capital" whose fringing reef is so accessible and protected it
is a model for reef management.

## Apo Reef, Philippines

The second-largest contiguous coral reef in the world, a marine natural park.

## Aldabra Atoll, Seychelles

A near-untouched UNESCO atoll, one of the largest raised coral atolls on Earth.

## Hecate Strait Glass Sponge Reefs, Canada

Not coral at all — reefs built by glass sponges, an ecosystem thought extinct
for 40 million years, found alive off British Columbia.

## Red Sea reefs, Egypt

Famously vibrant and resilient reefs whose corals show unusual heat tolerance —
a possible refuge in a warming ocean.

## What "impressive" means underwater

Scale stuns (the Great Barrier Reef), diversity overwhelms (Raja Ampat),
rarity astonishes (the glass sponge reefs), and resilience now matters most
(the Red Sea). The greatest reefs are wonders and warnings at the same time.

## Visiting the icons responsibly

These reefs are under extreme pressure from warming seas and tourism. Many
restrict numbers, require permits or liveaboards (Tubbataha, Aldabra), and
enforce strict no-contact rules. Dive with conservation-led operators, follow
every rule, and treat low impact as the price of admission — these places may
not survive a careless generation.
