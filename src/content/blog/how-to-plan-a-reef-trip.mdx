A reef trip is dictated by water — its temperature, clarity, and movement.
Plan around the marine season and your in-water ability and the rest follows.

## Step 1: Match the reef to your ability

Be honest: snorkeller, beginner diver, or experienced diver. Warm shallow
reefs suit everyone; offshore walls and cold-water sites are for trained
divers. Choosing a reef beyond your level is the most common — and most
dangerous — planning mistake.

## Step 2: Get the season right

Reefs have seasons: tropical reefs have calmer "dry" windows and storm seasons;
temperate and cold-water reefs are often clearest in winter; some sites peak
for specific events (spawning, manta or whale-shark aggregations). Research the
clearest, calmest window for your specific reef and build around it.

## Step 3: Sort certification early

If the trip involves scuba, get certified at home, not in a rushed resort
course. Refresh skills if you have not dived recently, and log the dives —
many top sites require proof of experience or an advanced card. Cold-water
reefs need drysuit training specifically.

## Step 4: Choose operators carefully

Pick dive and snorkel operators with strong safety records and explicit
conservation practice — mooring buoys not anchors, reef-safe policies, small
groups, briefings. The cheapest boat is often the one that damages the reef
and cuts safety corners. Read recent reviews for both.

## Step 5: Map and cluster the sites

Open the [map](/map), filter to your region, and group reefs reachable from one
base. A single liveaboard or shore base covering several nearby sites beats
chasing scattered reefs. Note depth and exposure on each.

## Step 6: Plan the daily rhythm

Reef diving and snorkelling are best in the calm, clear morning before wind and
crowds. Plan one or two water sessions a day with proper surface intervals;
respect no-fly times after diving when arranging departure flights.

## Step 7: Pack reef-safe

Mineral (reef-safe) sunscreen or, better, a rash guard and skin suit, a
well-fitting mask tested before the trip, and any personal gear you trust.
Chemical sunscreens are banned at many reefs and harm coral everywhere.

## Step 8: Travel low-impact

Maintain buoyancy, never touch or stand on coral, take nothing living or dead,
keep distance from wildlife, and follow all marine-park rules. Reefs are under
severe pressure from heat and tourism; a careful visitor is part of the
solution.

## Put it together

Ability, then season, then certification, then operators, then a clustered
base. Open the [map](/map), pin the sites, lock the season, and a reef trip
becomes safe, ethical, and unforgettable.
