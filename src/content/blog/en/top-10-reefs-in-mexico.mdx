Mexico's reef diving spans two oceans: the Caribbean coast from Cancun to the
Belizean border, with its barrier reef, cenotes, and world-famous Cozumel; and
the Sea of Cortez and Pacific coast, where cold-water upwellings produce
completely different ecological conditions. The Yucatan Peninsula also provides
an unusual complement to conventional reef diving: a network of freshwater and
halocline cenotes — submerged limestone cave systems — that connect to the
coastal reef ecosystem. All sites are on the [interactive map](/map).

## 1. Palancar Reef, Cozumel

Palancar Reef on Cozumel's southwestern coast is the crown of Mexican reef
diving: a series of coral formations — Palancar Bricks, Palancar Caves, Palancar
Garden — developed along a wall that drops from five metres to beyond forty, all
drifted on the consistent north-flowing Cozumel Current. Black coral Antipathes
caribbeana decorates the deeper sections; large sea fans Gorgonia ventalina
dominate the mid-wall. French angelfish Pomacanthus paru, black grouper
Mycteroperca bonaci, and Nassau grouper Epinephelus striatus are resident. Best
season: November to April.

## 2. Santa Rosa Wall and Columbia, Cozumel

Santa Rosa Wall is Cozumel's longest continuous drift site, a near-vertical reef
face that begins in three metres of water and is still rich in coral at thirty.
Columbia is the deepest drift site on the island, reaching into technical depth
but with exceptional formations accessible to recreational divers at twenty-five
metres. Both are drifted rather than swum; the current delivers divers along the
reef at consistent speed. Best season: November to April.

## 3. Punta Sur Reef, Cozumel

The southern tip of Cozumel has a reef complex that includes Dedos (Fingers) —
a series of coral fingers with corridors between them — and Punta Sur itself, where
the reef top in three to five metres of water combines current, hard coral, and
a resident population of green moray eels Gymnothorax funebris. Black grouper
and snapper school on the reef flat. Best season: November to May.

## 4. Akumal, Riviera Maya

Akumal — "place of turtles" in Mayan — is a shallow bay with a fringing reef where
green turtles Chelonia mydas graze on seagrass and hawksbill turtles Eretmochelys
imbricata feed on reef sponges. It is one of the most reliably turtle-dense
snorkel sites in the Caribbean. Visitor management protocols have been introduced
to limit the number of swimmers near turtles simultaneously, following research
that showed high visitor density was altering turtle behaviour. Best season:
May to September.

## 5. MUSA Underwater Museum, Cancun

The Museo Subacuatico de Arte (MUSA) is an installation of over 500 large
cement sculptures on the sandy seabed off Cancun and Isla Mujeres, at depths of
four to nine metres. The sculptures, designed to have pH-neutral surfaces
hospitable to coral settlement, have been colonised by hard and soft corals
over more than a decade and now support reef fish communities. They function
simultaneously as artificial reef, art installation, and diver-attraction that
relieves pressure from nearby natural reefs. Best season: November to April.

## 6. Dos Ojos and Angelita Cenotes, Riviera Maya

The cenotes of the Yucatan Peninsula are flooded limestone cave systems formed
when the peninsula's bedrock dissolved over millennia. Dos Ojos (Two Eyes) is
an accessible cavern system near Tulum with two large open chambers (cavern
diving does not require cave certification). Angelita is a single open cenote
with a dramatic halocline at thirty metres where freshwater above and saltwater
below create a cloud-like interface. Neither is a coral reef, but both connect
hydrologically to the reef ecosystem at the coast. Best season: Year-round.

## 7. Isla Mujeres Whale Sharks

From June to September, whale sharks Rhincodon typus aggregate in the open
water between Isla Mujeres and Holbox in the Yucatan Channel, feeding on the
spawn of cubera snapper and little tunny. This is the world's largest known
seasonal aggregation of whale sharks, with hundreds of individuals documented
in peak years. Snorkeling with whale sharks is the primary activity; permits
and guide requirements are in place to limit contact per individual shark.
Best season: June to September.

## 8. Banco Chinchorro, Quintana Roo

Banco Chinchorro is Mexico's largest atoll, an isolated reef system sixty
kilometres off the southern Quintana Roo coast that is accessible by a three-hour
boat trip from Mahahual or Xcalak. It is a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve. The atoll's
inner lagoon contains multiple historic wrecks. American crocodiles Crocodylus
acutus inhabit the mangrove cayes. The outer reef wall has Caribbean reef sharks,
tarpon, and hawksbill turtles. Best season: February to May.

## 9. Espiritu Santo, Sea of Cortez

Espiritu Santo Island, off La Paz in the Sea of Cortez, is a UNESCO Biosphere
Reserve with a rocky reef and sandy habitat that supports a completely different
fauna from Caribbean Mexico: sea lions Zalophus californianus dive alongside
visiting divers, Galapagos sharks Carcharhinus galapagensis patrol the outer
rock faces, and large schools of hammerheads Sphyrna lewini appear in summer.
Mobula rays in spectacular schools are a seasonal highlight. Best season:
June to November for pelagics.

## 10. Cabo Pulmo, Baja California Sur

Cabo Pulmo is the recovery story of Mexican marine conservation. After severe
depletion by overfishing through the 1990s, community-led closure to fishing
in 1995 and formal protected area designation in 1995 as a National Marine Park
have produced one of the most dramatic documented reef recoveries on record.
Biomass of predatory fish increased by nearly 500 percent in ten years. Bull
sharks Carcharhinus leucas, jack fish schools, and grouper are now abundant on
a reef that was nearly empty thirty years ago. Best season: May to November.
