Encountering a shark on a reef dive is, for the vast majority of divers, one of
the most compelling moments in the water. It is also frequently misread. Sharks
are not random predators waiting for an opportunity; they are animals with
legible behaviour, defined territories, and predictable responses to diver
presence. Understanding both which species you are likely to meet and what their
body language communicates turns an anxiety-provoking encounter into an
informative one.

## Blacktip reef shark

The blacktip reef shark Carcharhinus melanopterus is the species most divers
encounter first. Slim, fast, and unmistakable with the black-tipped dorsal and
caudal fins that give it its name, it is abundant on shallow reef flats and in
passes throughout the Indo-Pacific and parts of the Red Sea. Blacktips are
skittish; they typically circle at a distance and retreat if a diver approaches
directly. They are active predators of small reef fish and are not considered
a meaningful threat to divers. At sites like Fakarava or Rangiroa in French
Polynesia, hundreds of blacktips aggregate in passes during tidal exchanges,
producing some of the most photographed shark encounters in the world.

## Whitetip reef shark

The whitetip reef shark Triaenodon obesus is stockier and slower than the
blacktip and is commonly found resting in caves, under coral overhangs, and on
sandy patches during the day. It is a benthic hunter, active primarily at night
when it uses electroreception and scent to find reef fish in crevices. Divers
frequently find whitetips at the Cod Hole at the Great Barrier Reef, at Osprey
Reef, and throughout the Indo-Pacific. Resting whitetips appear indifferent to
diver presence and rarely move. They are not aggressive and are among the safest
of all reef shark species to observe.

## Grey reef shark

The grey reef shark Carcharhinus amblyrhynchos is the species most likely to
display the body language associated with threat in a reef context. When it feels
cornered — particularly in a confined channel or when multiple divers approach
simultaneously — it may perform a distinctive agonistic display: hunching the
back, dropping the pectoral fins, and swimming with an exaggerated lateral
movement. This display is a clear communication to increase distance. The
appropriate response is to back off slowly and give the shark room. Grey reef
sharks are present at most Indo-Pacific reef systems and are the dominant shark
at many Micronesian and Polynesian passes.

## Nurse shark

The nurse shark Ginglymostoma cirratum in the Atlantic and Caribbean and the
tawny nurse shark Nebrius ferrugineus in the Indo-Pacific are sluggish bottom
dwellers with small mouths adapted for extracting invertebrates and small fish
from crevices. They are frequently found piled on top of each other in shallow
reef caves during daylight. Nurse sharks are almost always non-reactive to
divers, but the small number of documented bites involving nurse sharks nearly
all involve a diver grabbing or cornering the animal. The rule applies: do not
touch them.

## Caribbean reef shark and lemon shark

The Caribbean reef shark Carcharhinus perezi is the most common large reef shark
in the western Atlantic and Caribbean. It is more robust than its Indo-Pacific
counterparts and, in areas without shark feeding, behaves with typical reef
shark wariness. The lemon shark Negaprion brevirostris is found on both Atlantic
and Pacific coasts and tends to occupy shallower, lagoonal environments. Both
species are featured prominently at Bahamas dive sites.

## Fiji's Beqa Lagoon

Beqa Lagoon, off the southern coast of Viti Levu in Fiji, hosts a structured
shark dive that aggregates bull sharks Carcharhinus leucas — one of the few large,
potentially dangerous species regularly encountered on recreational dives — along
with tawny nurse sharks, whitetip reef sharks, grey reef sharks, and occasionally
tiger sharks Galeocerdo cuvier. The dive is conducted at around thirty metres on
a rubble slope, with trained safety divers and a specific protocol for feeding.
It is one of the most intensively managed shark-encounter dives in the world.
Whether managed shark feeding is conservation-positive remains debated, but
the Beqa operation has been running since 2004 with no serious incidents.

## Bahamas Tiger Beach

Tiger Beach on the Little Bahama Bank, north of Grand Bahama Island, is the
world's most reliable location for shallow encounters with tiger sharks. Water
depth is typically six to ten metres. Tiger sharks Galeocerdo cuvier arrive
reliably from late October through January. Dives are conducted as a non-cage
surface-supplied or scuba experience, with multiple sharks present simultaneously.
Great hammerheads Sphyrna mokarran also appear at Tiger Beach, and Caribbean reef
sharks are present throughout. The encounter is open-water; standard protocol
is to kneel or stand on the sand and let the sharks approach rather than
pursuing them.

## Fakarava South Pass, French Polynesia

The southern pass of Fakarava Atoll, a UNESCO Biosphere Reserve, produces one
of the most extraordinary predatory events observable by divers: the grouper
aggregation. Each year, during winter full moons, thousands of marbled grouper
Epinephelus polyphekadion gather to spawn in the pass. Hundreds of grey reef
sharks coordinate to prey on the aggregation in what is sometimes described as a
"wall of sharks" hunting formation. Outside spawning season, the pass holds
resident populations of blacktip reef sharks, whitetip reef sharks, and grey
reef sharks that can number in the hundreds during tidal exchanges.

## Encountering hammerheads

Great hammerheads Sphyrna mokarran and scalloped hammerheads Sphyrna lewini
are more shy than their reputation suggests. At schools sites like the Galapagos,
Cocos Island, and Yonaguni in Japan, hammerheads typically hang at the edge of
visibility and require patient, slow-moving observation. Approaching directly
causes them to retreat. Staying still, making yourself small, and allowing the
school to slowly close in is more effective than any active approach. Great
hammerheads at Tiger Beach and Bimini are different — larger, solitary, and
capable of investigating divers at close range. The same principle applies:
slow movement, no sudden gestures, no direct charge.
