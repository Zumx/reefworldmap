The Belize Barrier Reef Reserve System is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and the
second-longest barrier reef in the world after Australia's Great Barrier Reef.
Running 300 kilometres along the Caribbean coast of Belize, it is joined by
three offshore atolls — Lighthouse Reef, Glover's Reef, and Turneffe Atoll —
to form one of the most complete and varied reef ecosystems in the western
Atlantic. Combined fish diversity, coral health on the outer atolls, and the
accessibility of several sites to snorkelers make it Central America's most
significant marine destination. All sites are on the [interactive map](/map).

## 1. Great Blue Hole, Lighthouse Reef Atoll

The Blue Hole is a marine sinkhole 300 metres across and 125 metres deep, formed
during the last ice age when sea levels were lower. It is a barrier reef atoll
at the outer edge of the Belize system and is primarily a wall and depth dive:
the interior walls are colonised by stalactite formations and populated by
Caribbean reef sharks Carcharhinus perezi, blacktip sharks, and the occasional
hammerhead at depth. Coral diversity is lower in the deep interior than on
surrounding atoll reef. Best for experienced divers. Best season: December to
April.

## 2. Lighthouse Reef Atoll

The atoll that contains the Blue Hole is itself a world-class reef system.
Half Moon Caye Wall, on the southeastern edge, is a sheer drop-off with dense
soft coral growth and excellent visibility, and the island above is a protected
booby nesting site managed by the Belize Audubon Society. Tarpon, hawksbill
turtles Eretmochelys imbricata, and Nassau grouper Epinephelus striatus are
resident. The fringing reef inside the atoll is accessible for snorkeling. Best
season: November to April.

## 3. Half Moon Caye, Lighthouse Reef

The southern wall of Half Moon Caye combines a vertical drop with a sandy
terrace at thirty metres that holds sleeping nurse sharks Ginglymostoma cirratum
and eagle rays Aetobatus narinari. The island itself is a Natural Monument
with a nesting colony of red-footed boobies visible from a viewing platform.
Combining an atoll wall dive with wildlife watching above water makes this a
genuinely all-round stop. Best season: January to May.

## 4. Glover's Reef Atoll

Glover's Reef is the most offshore of the three Belize atolls and one of the
best-preserved reef systems in the Caribbean. A no-take marine reserve covers
its southern section. The interior patch reef complex has dense hard coral cover
and unusual fish density; the outer walls have shark and ray populations little
affected by fishing pressure. Nassau grouper Epinephelus striatus still aggregate
for spawning at specific moon phases in winter — an event nearly absent from
heavily fished Caribbean reefs. Best season: December to March.

## 5. Turneffe Atoll

Turneffe is the largest atoll in Belize and the closest to Belize City, making
it the most accessible for day trips. The Elbow, its southern tip, is one of
the Caribbean's premier wall dives: a strong outgoing current sweeps divers
along a wall colonised by black coral and large sea fans, with eagle rays,
turtles, and Caribbean reef sharks routinely visible. The interior of the atoll
has extensive mangrove habitat that serves as nursery ground for the reef fish
population. Best season: November to May.

## 6. Hol Chan Marine Reserve

Hol Chan, a short boat ride from Ambergris Caye, is the oldest marine reserve
in Belize and one of the most visited. A channel cut through the barrier reef
creates a tidal current that aggregates fish in extraordinary concentrations.
Goliath grouper Epinephelus itajara, several species of snapper, and large
schools of grunts are visible from the surface. Nurse sharks are present on
the sandy floor. Snorkeling-accessible. Best season: November to April.

## 7. Shark Ray Alley, Hol Chan

Shark Ray Alley is a specific site within the Hol Chan Reserve where a history
of fish cleaning by local fishermen has created a permanent aggregation of nurse
sharks Ginglymostoma cirratum and southern stingrays Hypanus americanus. Snorkelers
and divers hover over the sand while sharks and rays circle below. The site is
one of the most visited in Belize and can be crowded; early morning visits
produce a more composed experience. Best season: December to April.

## 8. South Water Caye Marine Reserve

South Water Caye sits on the inner barrier reef south of Belize City and protects
both reef and seagrass habitat. The combination is ecologically significant:
the seagrass beds immediately behind the reef support manatee Trichechus manatus
populations and serve as foraging habitat for green turtles. The reef itself
has been less impacted by tourism pressure than northern sites and offers
consistently good coral cover and fish life. Best season: February to May.

## 9. Sapodilla Cayes

The Sapodilla Cayes are the southernmost reef area in the Belize system, near
the Guatemalan and Honduran borders. The Marine Reserve here is less visited
than northern Belize sites and offers a less commercialised reef experience.
Coral cover is high on the outer faces; whale sharks Rhincodon typus pass
through in season during spring. The cayes themselves are remote sandy islands
with limited accommodation. Best season: February to June.

## 10. Bacalar Chico Marine Reserve, Ambergris Caye

At the northern tip of Ambergris Caye, Bacalar Chico protects the barrier reef
alongside a UNESCO-listed Maya archaeological site at the water's edge. Manatees
Trichechus manatus, crocodiles, and a healthy mangrove network are inland; the
outer reef face has good soft coral growth and American saltwater crocodile
Crocodylus acutus are occasionally seen near mangrove margins. A reef system
with rare combined archaeological and marine conservation significance. Best
season: December to March.
