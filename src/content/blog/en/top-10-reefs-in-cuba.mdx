Cuba's reefs have been partly insulated from the overfishing and coastal
development pressures that have degraded much of the wider Caribbean by a
combination of economic restrictions on fishing activity and the limited
infrastructure that has historically constrained mass tourism. The result is
a reef system that retains fish densities largely absent elsewhere in the
Caribbean, including large predatory species that have been effectively fished
out across most of the region. International access has improved steadily and
Cuba now offers some of the most compelling reef diving in the western Atlantic.

## 1. Jardines de la Reina, Ciego de Avila Province

Jardines de la Reina — Gardens of the Queen — is Cuba's flagship marine
protected area, a no-take zone covering 840 square kilometres of reef, mangrove,
and seagrass in the southern Caribbean. Commercial and recreational fishing are
prohibited throughout. The result is reef fish biomass that researchers have
documented at five to ten times the Caribbean average: large schools of cubera
snapper Lutjanus cyanopterus, intact grouper populations, American crocodile
Crocodylus acutus in the mangrove shallows, and one of the Caribbean's most
reliable populations of Caribbean reef sharks Carcharhinus perezi. Access is by
liveaboard only, departing from Jucaro. Best season: October to June.

## 2. Maria la Gorda, Pinar del Rio Province

At the westernmost tip of Cuba, Maria la Gorda sits at the entrance to the Gulf
of Mexico on the Bahia de Corrientes. The reef drop-off begins just metres from
the beach, making shore entry possible for experienced divers. The site has
exceptional black coral growth at depth and consistent encounters with eagle
rays Aetobatus narinari and large tarpon Megalops atlanticus. The area is less
visited than northern Cuba dive sites and retains strong fish populations. Best
season: November to May.

## 3. Cayo Largo del Sur

Cayo Largo is a tourist island off Cuba's southwestern coast with fringing reefs
accessible by day boat. The reefs here are less protected than Jardines de la
Reina but benefit from the relative isolation of the southern cays. Hawksbill
turtle Eretmochelys imbricata nesting occurs on the island beaches, and turtles
are reliably seen on the reefs. Green turtles Chelonia mydas are also common.
The reef fish populations include large French angelfish Pomacanthus paru and
schools of grunts. Best season: December to April.

## 4. Punta Frances, Isla de la Juventud

The southwestern tip of Isla de la Juventud (Isle of Youth) is protected within
the Punta Frances National Marine Park. The wall at Punta Frances is considered
by many Cuban dive professionals to be the most dramatic vertical structure in
Cuban waters, dropping from eight to fifty metres with dense sea fan and
sponge growth. Caribbean reef sharks patrol the wall, and the mangrove channels
behind the reef hold American crocodiles. Liveaboard or day trips from Nueva
Gerona. Best season: December to May.

## 5. Isla de la Juventud Reefs

Beyond Punta Frances, Isla de la Juventud has an extensive reef system along
its southern coast, most of it protected within Punta Frances Marine Park.
Multiple dive sites along the southern wall offer encounters with silky sharks
Carcharhinus falciformis, manta rays Mobula birostris, and large grouper. The
island's interior hosts one of the Caribbean's largest crocodile populations.
Best season: November to May.

## 6. Cayo Coco, Ciego de Avila Province

Cayo Coco on Cuba's northern coast has reef systems accessible from the
all-inclusive hotel zone, making it the most convenient Cuban reef experience
for mainstream tourists. Coral cover is lower than southern Cuba sites and
fishing pressure is higher, but the reefs are productive and the warm northern
Caribbean water makes it accessible year-round. Spiny lobster Panulirus argus
are abundant. Best season: November to April.

## 7. Guardalavaca, Holguin Province

Guardalavaca on the northeastern coast is Cuba's second tourism zone. The
adjacent reefs have been less studied than western Cuba sites but benefit from
the relative shelter of the reef lagoon. Octopus vulgaris are common, and the
muck-diving equivalent of the area produces interesting macro life including
frogfish Antennarius sp. Best season: December to April.

## 8. Bahia de Cochinos (Bay of Pigs)

The Bay of Pigs on Cuba's southwestern coast is notable outside diving circles
for its 1961 history, but its reefs are productive and surprisingly varied.
The reef wall at Punta Gorda drops sharply from a shallow reef flat colonised
by brain corals Diploria strigosa and star corals Orbicella annularis to a
wall with good sponge and soft coral growth. Tarpon schools aggregate in
the bay itself. Accessibility has improved with expanding dive operator
infrastructure. Best season: December to April.

## 9. Santiago de Cuba Region

The southeastern coast of Cuba near Santiago has reef systems that remain
under-documented relative to western Cuba. The waters here are influenced by
Caribbean current mixing and tend to have good visibility. The region's relative
isolation from tourism infrastructure has limited diving development but
preserved fish populations. Reef composition includes both hard and soft corals
with good turtle densities. Best season: December to May.

## 10. Rio Cuyaguateje, Pinar del Rio

The reef systems at the western end of Cuba near the Rio Cuyaguateje estuary
include mangrove-backed fringing reefs with conditions characteristic of
transition zones between freshwater and marine environments. Tarpon, snook
Centropomus undecimalis, and barracuda Sphyraena barracuda aggregate at the
mangrove edges, with Caribbean reef fish communities on the outer reef faces.
Best season: November to April.
