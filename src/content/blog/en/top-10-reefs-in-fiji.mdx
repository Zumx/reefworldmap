Fiji sits at the convergence of several major Pacific current systems, and the
nutrient-rich water that these currents bring has produced reef ecosystems
of extraordinary richness in soft coral, invertebrate life, and fish biomass.
The Somosomo Strait between Taveuni and Vanua Levu is the most celebrated zone,
but the Bligh Water, the Astrolabe Reef, and the outer island groups each
deliver distinct experiences. Fiji's liveaboard industry is well developed,
and many of the best sites are accessible from shore or by day boat from
established dive bases. All sites are on the [interactive map](/map).

## 1. Beqa Lagoon, Viti Levu

Beqa Lagoon is the site of Fiji's most acclaimed shark dive, running from
the Shark Reef Marine Reserve on the lagoon's inner edge. Bull sharks
Carcharhinus leucas are the headline species, with adults aggregating at
a rubble slope at around thirty metres in numbers that can exceed fifteen
individuals on a single dive. Tawny nurse sharks Nebrius ferrugineus, whitetip
reef sharks, and grey reef sharks accompany the bull sharks, and tiger sharks
Galeocerdo cuvier make seasonal appearances. The Reserve is a community-managed
area; dive fees support both conservation and the surrounding village. Best
season: May to October.

## 2. Rainbow Reef, Somosomo Strait, Taveuni

Rainbow Reef runs along the Taveuni shore of the Somosomo Strait and is
considered the soft coral capital of the world, with dense growth of
Dendronephthya soft corals in purples, oranges, and whites that open fully
only in current. The Great White Wall is its most iconic site: a vertical
face beginning at around eighteen metres covered entirely in white soft coral.
Reef fish diversity is exceptional, with hundreds of species in a single dive.
Current can be strong; timing entry to the tidal phase is critical. Best season:
May to October.

## 3. Bligh Water, Northern Viti Levu

Bligh Water is the open ocean zone between the northern Fijian islands and a
liveaboard destination rather than a shore-accessible dive area. Named for the
stretch William Bligh navigated after the Bounty mutiny, it contains pinnacles
and seamounts with currents that concentrate pelagic life. Hammerhead sharks
Sphyrna lewini, manta rays Mobula alfredi, and large schools of barracuda and
trevally are the main draws. The site E6 is a well-known pinnacle in Bligh Water
with dramatic soft coral growth and shark encounters. Best season: July to
October.

## 4. Astrolabe Reef, Kadavu

The Astrolabe Reef, surrounding Kadavu Island south of Viti Levu, is a barrier
reef enclosing a large lagoon in a horseshoe shape. The outer wall has
outstanding hard coral growth and a higher proportion of large table corals
Acropora sp. than the northern Fiji reefs. Manta ray cleaning stations are
active on the reef's northern section. The limited number of dive operators
on Kadavu means the reef sees significantly less traffic than Taveuni and Viti
Levu sites. Best season: April to October.

## 5. E6, Bligh Water

E6 is a specific seamount in Bligh Water surrounded by nutrient-rich upwelling
water. The pinnacle top at around twelve metres is colonised by hard corals, but
it is the mid-water column that draws divers: circling hammerhead sharks,
dogtooth tuna Gymnosarda unicolor, and marlin transit the site when currents are
running. Visibility can reach forty metres in calm conditions. Liveaboard only.
Best season: July to September.

## 6. Great White Wall, Taveuni

The Great White Wall is a site within the Rainbow Reef complex distinct enough
in character to warrant separate mention. The approach is through a natural arch
at around seventeen metres before a thermocline drop delivers divers to the white
soft coral wall. The white coloration is the open-expanded form of Dendronephthya;
the same corals appear translucent or orange when contracted at slack water.
Below the white wall, at twenty-five to thirty metres, hard coral and seafans
dominate. One of Fiji's signature photographic sites. Best season: May to
September.

## 7. Vatu-i-Ra Passage, Northern Viti Levu

The Vatu-i-Ra Passage connects Bligh Water to the Koro Sea and generates
strong tidal flows past a series of coral pinnacles and submerged reefs. The
passage is known for bumphead parrotfish Bolbometopon muricatum — schools of
up to a hundred individuals feeding on coral at dawn — and schools of
chevron barracuda Sphyraena genie. The passage is also used by spinner
dolphins Stenella longirostris. Best season: June to September.

## 8. Wakaya, Lomaiviti Group

Wakaya is a private island in the Lomaiviti Group accessible by liveaboard or
light aircraft, with a small number of dive operators. Its outer reef walls are
exceptional for large pelagic encounters: manta rays Mobula birostris, hammerhead
sharks, and occasionally whale sharks Rhincodon typus. The protected status of
the Wakaya marine area and low diver traffic contribute to outstanding fish
biomass on the reef. Best season: July to October.

## 9. Yasawa Islands

The Yasawa chain runs sixty kilometres northwest of Viti Levu and is reached by
fast ferry or light aircraft. The outer reef faces have good hard coral
communities, and the Sawa-i-Lau limestone sea caves at the northern end combine
snorkeling with dramatic above-water cave formations. Mantas are present at
several seasonal cleaning stations. Best season: May to October.

## 10. Coral spawning timing, Fiji-wide

Fiji's annual coral spawning event occurs typically in late November to early
December, triggered by the full moon following the water temperature reaching
approximately 27 degrees Celsius. The event is a mass broadcast spawning where
staghorn, plate, and brain corals release pink and cream egg-sperm bundles
simultaneously. Divers who time their visit to Beqa or the Somosomo Strait for
the spawning period witness the reef's most intimate biological event. Coral
larvae settle on reef substrate over the following weeks. Best season: Late
November.
