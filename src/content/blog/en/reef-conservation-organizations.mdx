Coral reefs face stressors that no single actor can address alone. Climate
science, hands-on restoration, water quality advocacy, fisheries management, and
the political work of securing marine protected areas require different expertise
and operate at different scales. This is a survey of the organisations doing
that work, from global scientific bodies to community-scale NGOs operating on
individual atolls.

## Australian Institute of Marine Science (AIMS)

AIMS is Australia's tropical marine research agency and the source of the most
comprehensive long-term dataset on Great Barrier Reef condition in existence. Its
annual Long-Term Monitoring Program, running since 1986, surveys coral cover,
reef fish populations, crown-of-thorns starfish densities, and bleaching extent
across hundreds of sites. When the scientific community assesses the 2016, 2017,
or 2024 bleaching events on the Great Barrier Reef, the primary field data come
from AIMS researchers. The institute also leads work on coral assisted evolution
— breeding heat-tolerant coral genotypes — and on integrating temperature
monitoring with NOAA Coral Reef Watch's Degree Heating Weeks alert system.

## Coral Restoration Foundation (CRF), Florida

Founded in 2007, CRF operates the world's largest coral restoration programme
in the Florida Reef Tract, the third-longest barrier reef system globally and a
system that has lost roughly ninety-eight percent of its staghorn and elkhorn
coral since the 1970s. CRF grows staghorn coral Acropora cervicornis and elkhorn
coral Acropora palmata on underwater nursery trees — pipe frames from which coral
fragments grow suspended in the water column — and then transplants outplanted
fragments to degraded reef sections. The foundation has planted more than a
hundred thousand corals to date and now trials multiple genotypes to identify
individuals with above-average heat tolerance for use in future restoration.

## Reef Check

Founded in 1996, Reef Check trains recreational divers and snorkellers to conduct
standardised reef health surveys using a fixed protocol that generates data
comparable across sites and years. Volunteers count indicator fish species — such
as humphead wrasse Cheilinus undulatus and bumphead parrotfish Bolbometopon
muricatum — and invertebrates including diadema sea urchins and giant clams, all
of which serve as proxies for fishing pressure and reef health. The organisation
now has trained surveyors in more than ninety countries and produces one of the
few globally consistent long-term citizen science datasets on reef condition.

## SECORE International

SECORE focuses on coral sexual reproduction as a restoration pathway. Wild corals
release eggs and sperm in brief, lunar-timed spawning events. SECORE researchers
collect gametes during these spawning events, facilitate fertilisation in
controlled conditions, and raise the resulting larvae until they are large enough
to be settled onto reef substrate using specially designed settlement devices. The
advantage over the fragmentation approach used in tree nurseries is genetic
diversity: sexually produced corals are not clones, and a diverse planted cohort
is more resilient to disease and environmental variability than a population of
genetically identical fragments.

## Reef Resilience Network

The Reef Resilience Network, coordinated through The Nature Conservancy, is not
a field programme but a practitioner learning platform. It hosts a library of
management tools, training materials, and case studies for reef managers —
particularly those running marine protected areas — covering topics from zoning
design to bleaching response protocols. For a park manager in a Pacific island
nation with limited resources and no access to a large research institution, the
network provides a practical connection to global best practice.

## CORDIO East Africa

The Coastal Oceans Research and Development in the Indian Ocean (CORDIO) focuses
on the Western Indian Ocean: Kenya, Tanzania, Mozambique, Madagascar, the
Seychelles, and Comoros. It combines reef monitoring with research into
communities whose food security and livelihoods depend directly on reef fish.
CORDIO's work on co-management — governance arrangements that give coastal
communities formal authority over reef areas — has influenced marine policy
across the region.

## WWF Coral Triangle Programme

The Coral Triangle — the marine area spanning Indonesia, Malaysia, the
Philippines, Papua New Guinea, the Solomon Islands, and Timor-Leste — contains
more coral species and more reef fish species than any comparable area on earth.
WWF operates a long-running programme in the region focused on expanding marine
protected area coverage, reducing destructive fishing practices including blast
fishing and cyanide fishing, and supporting sustainable small-scale fisheries.
Its network spans government, community, and private sector partners across all
six Coral Triangle countries.

## Reef Renewal Foundation Bonaire

Reef Renewal Foundation Bonaire is an example of a place-based restoration NGO
operating at the community scale. Bonaire's house reefs, which ring the island
and are accessible from the shore, have experienced staghorn and elkhorn coral
decline consistent with the wider Caribbean pattern. Reef Renewal trains local
divers, hotel staff, and visitors in coral nursery work and outplanting, creating
a volunteer pipeline that has planted tens of thousands of corals on Bonaire's
fringing reefs. The model — tying a dive destination's tourism identity to active
reef restoration — has been replicated in Curacao, Sint Maarten, and several
other Caribbean islands.

## Indo-Pacific MPA networks

Across the Indo-Pacific, networks of marine protected areas have been established
with support from bilateral aid programmes, the Global Environment Facility, and
international conservation organisations. The Micronesian Challenge committed
Palau, the Federated States of Micronesia, the Marshall Islands, Guam, and the
Northern Mariana Islands to protecting thirty percent of their near-shore marine
resources. The Locally Managed Marine Area Network (LMMA) in the Pacific
coordinates community-based no-take zones across Fiji, Solomon Islands, Vanuatu,
and Papua New Guinea. These community-governed areas have in some cases produced
measurable recoveries in target fish species within five to ten years of
establishment.

## What connects them

Different in scale and method, these organisations share two convictions: that
reefs cannot be restored without the engagement of the communities living
alongside them, and that local conservation gains — however significant — are
conditional on the global emissions reductions needed to keep sea temperatures
within a range corals can survive.
