Tourism is both one of the strongest arguments for protecting coral reefs and
one of the pressures that degrades them. A healthy reef generates income through
diving, snorkelling, and fisheries that dwarfs the short-term value of
destructive alternatives. But that same flow of visitors — if poorly managed —
inflicts cumulative physical damage, chemical contamination, and behavioural
disturbance that compounds the stresses already imposed by warming and pollution.
Responsible reef tourism is not a niche concern; it is the difference between
a reef that remains worth visiting and one that does not.

## Sunscreen and reef-safe chemistry

Research published since 2008 has confirmed that oxybenzone (benzophenone-3)
and octinoxate (ethylhexyl methoxycinnamate), two ultraviolet filters common in
conventional sunscreens, cause measurable harm to coral larvae and zooxanthellae
at concentrations found in heavily visited reef waters. Hawaii and Palau have
enacted legislation banning personal care products containing these compounds
for use near the ocean. The practical alternative is mineral sunscreen using
zinc oxide or titanium dioxide as the UV-blocking agent. Zinc oxide is
photostable, does not penetrate skin in the same way as organic filters, and
does not disrupt coral endocrine systems at realistic environmental
concentrations. Wearing a rash guard and a hat removes the need for most
sunscreen application before entering the water.

## Buoyancy and the no-touch rule

A single careless fin kick that breaks a coral branch may destroy growth that
took ten years to accumulate. Coral is not rock; its surface is living tissue.
Contact with skin, fins, gloves, or equipment transmits bacteria, removes the
protective mucus layer, and creates entry points for disease. The rule is
absolute: do not touch the reef, the substrate, or any marine organism. This
requires neutral buoyancy, which means completing your buoyancy training before
diving reef sites. Peak Performance Buoyancy is a recognised PADI specialty that
specifically addresses control in reef environments; comparable training exists
under SSI, BSAC, and other agencies. Diving with too much weight, over-inflated
BCDs, or poor trim — all of which make contact with the reef more likely — are
operator and diver failures that responsible dive briefings address directly.

## Green Fins membership

Green Fins is a programme run by the Reef-World Foundation in partnership with
the United Nations Environment Programme. It provides a set of environmental
standards for dive and snorkel operators — covering equipment maintenance, waste
management, briefing quality, and reef interaction guidelines — and offers
membership to operators who pass audits conducted by trained local staff. The
Green Fins member database is publicly searchable. Choosing a Green Fins member
operator does not guarantee a perfect experience, but it provides a baseline
assurance that the operator has been assessed against documented environmental
standards and found to meet them.

## PADI Five Star and eco-dive programmes

PADI Five Star Dive Centres meet PADI's standards for instructor training ratios,
equipment standards, and business practice. Within that tier, PADI offers an
Eco Dive Centre designation for operators who meet additional environmental
criteria. Other agency equivalents — SSI Green Star, BSAC's environmental
awareness programmes — work similarly. When evaluating an operator, ask
specifically how their dive briefing covers reef interaction and what they do when
a diver makes contact with the reef. A strong operator stops the dive.

## Anchoring versus mooring buoys

A boat anchor dropped onto a reef crest can shatter coral cover across an area
of several square metres with each deployment. Sustained anchor damage over
years converts productive reef into rubble. Mooring buoys — concrete blocks or
screwed anchors fitted with a surface pickup line — allow boats to tie off
without touching the reef. Installation and maintenance programmes run by
national park authorities and NGOs have significantly reduced anchor damage at
many Caribbean and Indo-Pacific sites. Check whether your target site has
functioning moorings. If it does not, ask your operator whether they carry a
sand anchor designed for use away from coral.

## Feeding fish and shark-baiting debates

Feeding fish disrupts natural foraging behaviour, habituates fish to human
presence in ways that alter their risk responses, and at high volumes can shift
the species composition of a reef community by favouring opportunistic species
over those with more specialised diets. Structured shark-feeding dives — where
operators introduce food to concentrate sharks for tourist viewing — are more
controversial. Proponents argue that shark dives generate income that makes
sharks economically valuable and thus politically protected from fishing. Critics
argue that they create habituation, alter natural behaviour, and carry accident
risk both to divers and to sharks that may be caught after associating humans
with food. The balance of published evidence supports avoiding feeding any reef
organism on a recreational dive.

## Single-use plastic and boat waste

Plastic waste from dive boats contributes to the marine debris that smothers
reefs and entangles reef fish and turtles. Responsible operators carry reusable
water containers rather than single-use bottles, have a waste separation system
on board, and do not discharge food waste, bilge water, or grey water directly
over reef areas. Ask before you book whether the boat has a water refill station
and a system for separating recyclables.

## Carrying capacity and site rotation

Some reef sites receive more diver visits per day than the site ecology can
absorb without degradation. Responsible operators and park managers limit
simultaneous diver numbers at sensitive sites, rotate sites to allow recovery
periods, and maintain closed seasons for particularly fragile locations. Thailand's
Similan Islands National Park has instituted seasonal closures for reef recovery.
Palau's Nikko Bay limits daily visitor numbers. Choosing operators who respect
these limits — even when it means a smaller group or a less convenient time slot —
is a meaningful choice.
