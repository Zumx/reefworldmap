The Bahamas archipelago, spread across 300,000 square kilometres of shallow
Caribbean and Atlantic water, has a reef geography unlike anywhere else. Its
coral grows on a shallow bank system rather than a continental shelf, producing
clear, warm water and the combination of wall, reef flat, and sandy-bottom
habitats that create exceptional shark diving. The absence of strong agricultural
runoff — the islands are largely limestone with no river systems — means the
Bahamian reef benefits from clean, high-visibility water year-round. All sites
are on the [interactive map](/map).

## 1. Tiger Beach, Grand Bahama

Tiger Beach on the Little Bahama Bank in six to ten metres of clear, sandy-bottom
water is the world's most consistent location for encounters with tiger sharks
Galeocerdo cuvier. From October through January, multiple tiger sharks are
reliably present simultaneously; the shallow depth and excellent visibility make
it accessible to Open Water-certified divers. Great hammerheads Sphyrna mokarran
appear at the same site, along with Caribbean reef sharks Carcharhinus perezi and
lemon sharks Negaprion brevirostris. No cage is used; divers kneel or stand
on the sandy bottom. Best season: October to February.

## 2. Bimini

The Bimini Islands are the closest Bahamian islands to the Florida coast and
host year-round lemon shark Negaprion brevirostris populations, including a
nursery where juveniles use mangrove edges as shelter. Great hammerheads
Sphyrna mokarran appear off Bimini from December to April. The Bimini Road — a
linear series of large stone blocks on the seafloor that some interpret as a
pre-Columbian causeway — adds an archaeological dimension to the diving. Best
season: October to May.

## 3. Andros Barrier Reef

The reef running along the eastern edge of Andros Island is the third-longest
barrier reef in the world, at over 190 kilometres. The drop-off from shallow reef
flat to the Tongue of the Ocean — a 2,000-metre deep trench — is abrupt and
dramatic. The outer wall is heavily colonised by black coral at depth and large
sea fans at mid-range. Fish biomass is higher than most Bahamian reefs due to
Andros's sparse human population and limited commercial fishing. Best season:
December to April.

## 4. Stuart Cove, Nassau, New Providence

Stuart Cove's dive operation, south of Nassau, runs the best-organised shark
dive in the Nassau area: multiple Caribbean reef sharks Carcharhinus perezi on
a fifteen-metre sandy ledge, with trained safety divers and a feeding routine
that has been operating for decades. The site is a thirty-minute boat ride from
Nassau and is accessible to divers of all levels. Additional sites near Stuart
Cove include a DC-3 aircraft wreck at thirty metres colonised by sponges and
soft corals. Best season: Year-round.

## 5. Exuma Cays Land and Sea Park

The Exuma Cays Land and Sea Park, established in 1958, is one of the oldest
marine protected areas in the Caribbean and a no-take zone since 1986. The
reef systems within the park — at distances from Nassau that require a flight
or liveaboard — show fish biomass densities consistent with long-term no-take
protection. Nassau grouper Epinephelus striatus, a species that has been fished
to functional extinction across much of the Caribbean, are abundant and
approachable. Best season: December to April.

## 6. Lost Blue Hole, Andros

Blue holes in the Bahamas are vertical shafts in the limestone substrate that
connect the land surface or seafloor to a network of freshwater and saltwater
cave systems. The Lost Blue Hole off South Andros is an offshore marine blue
hole with halocline diving similar to Bahamian inland blue holes. The interior
walls are colonised by sponges, and nurse sharks Ginglymostoma cirratum rest
on the ledges. Cave and cavern certification required for interior penetration.
Best season: December to April.

## 7. Eleuthera

Eleuthera's eastern coast is exposed to open Atlantic swell, creating energy-
driven reef communities with good hard coral diversity. Current Hole, a famous
site near the Glass Window Bridge where the Atlantic and Caribbean are separated
by a strip of land only twenty metres wide, generates tidal races that produce
some of the strongest drift diving in the Bahamas. Schooling tuna, jacks, and
Caribbean reef sharks are regular. Best season: January to May.

## 8. Cay Sal Bank

Cay Sal Bank, in the narrow Florida Straits between the Bahamas and Cuba, is one
of the most isolated reef systems in the western Atlantic, accessible only by
liveaboard. The reef has seen very little diver traffic and retains fish
populations that reflect minimal fishing pressure: large grouper, intact shark
populations, and abundant Nassau grouper Epinephelus striatus. Best season:
April to June.

## 9. Long Island, Bahamas

Long Island has a wall called Dean's Blue Hole — the world's second-deepest known
marine blue hole at 202 metres — in a sheltered bay with a fringing reef nearby.
The blue hole itself is a free-diving venue rather than a scuba site but the
adjacent fringing reef and wall diving off Long Island's western shore is
consistently productive, with eagle rays Aetobatus narinari, green and hawksbill
turtles, and Caribbean reef sharks. Best season: December to April.

## 10. Pig Beach, Exumas (Big Major Cay)

Big Major Cay, known globally as Pig Beach for its swimming pigs, is not a coral
reef site but a cultural and natural landmark that anchors Exumas itineraries.
The beach and surrounding shallow waters have healthy seagrass and some fringing
reef on the outer edges where nurse sharks and rays are regular. Including it in
an Exumas itinerary alongside the Exuma Cays Land and Sea Park makes for a
complete experience of the archipelago's unusual ecology and character. Best
season: November to May.
