Japan's reef diving is concentrated in its southwestern island chains: Okinawa
and the Ryukyu Islands, stretching toward Taiwan, and the Bonin Islands
(Ogasawara) south of the main archipelago. These subtropical and tropical
systems sit at the northern edge of the Indo-Pacific reef province and combine
hard coral communities with pelagic encounters — particularly schooling
scalloped hammerheads Sphyrna lewini — that are among the most spectacular in
the Pacific. Japan's dive infrastructure is well organised and the islands are
served by regular domestic flights from Tokyo and Osaka. All sites are on the
[interactive map](/map).

## 1. Kerama Islands, Okinawa

The Kerama Islands, a thirty-minute ferry west of Naha, form a designated
national park with some of Okinawa's clearest water and best-preserved reef
systems. The "Kerama blue" — exceptional underwater visibility — is a phrase
used throughout Japanese diving. Hard coral cover on the outer reef faces is
high; inner reef zones have significant branching Acropora. Loggerhead turtles
Caretta caretta are present year-round and are exceptionally unafraid of divers.
Humpback whales Megaptera novaeangliae pass through in winter. Best season:
May to October.

## 2. Yonaguni, Okinawa (Westernmost Ryukyus)

Yonaguni is the westernmost island of Japan, closer to Taiwan than to Okinawa,
and is famous for two things: the contested Yonaguni Monument — a series of
underwater rock formations that are either natural erosion or ancient construction
— and the winter aggregation of scalloped hammerhead sharks Sphyrna lewini that
congregates from December to April when cold Kuroshio Current water pushes
through. Schools of up to a hundred hammerheads at depths of twenty to thirty
metres make this one of the most reliable hammerhead encounters in Asia. Best
season: December to April for hammerheads.

## 3. Ishigaki, Manta Scramble

Ishigaki Island hosts one of the most reliably concentrated manta ray encounters
in the world at a site called Manta Scramble (Kabira Ishizaki area). Resident
oceanic mantas Mobula birostris and reef mantas Mobula alfredi gather at cleaning
stations where cleaner wrasse Labroides dimidiatus remove parasites. Up to twenty
mantas can be present simultaneously in peak season. The site is accessible
by short boat trip from Ishigaki port. Snorkeling is productive on calm days.
Best season: June to November.

## 4. Iriomote-Ishigaki National Park

The national park encompassing Iriomote Island and Ishigaki Island is one of
Japan's most biodiverse protected areas, with reef systems supporting over
360 coral species. Iriomote's rivers drain into mangrove systems that transition
to fringing reef at the coast; sea kayaking through the mangroves combined with
reef snorkeling is a distinctive way to experience the park's ecological variety.
Rare Iriomote cats Prionailurus bengalensis iriomotensis live in the forest.
Best season: May to September.

## 5. Onna Village, Okinawa Main Island

Onna Village on Okinawa's central west coast hosts the densest concentration of
dive operators on the island, with quick access to several productive reef sites
including the channel dives around Cape Maeda. The cape's underwater arch at
fifteen metres is one of the most photographed dive sites in Japan. Hard coral
cover has recovered from bleaching events and the fish populations include large
bumphead parrotfish Bolbometopon muricatum at several sites. Best season:
May to October.

## 6. Mibaru Beach, Okinawa Main Island

Mibaru Beach is Okinawa's most popular shore-entry snorkel site, on the island's
southeastern coast near Nanjo City. The shallow reef flat immediately off the
beach has dense staghorn coral Acropora sp., schools of convict surgeonfish
Acanthurus triostegus, and regular encounters with green turtles Chelonia mydas
at the reef's outer edge. Suitable for beginners and families. Best season:
May to September.

## 7. Sesoko Island, Okinawa

Sesoko, reached by bridge from Okinawa's northwest coast, has a sheltered reef
on its eastern side that is popular for night diving. The nocturnal reef is
notably active: hunting lionfish Pterois volitans, hermit crabs, sleeping
parrotfish in their mucus cocoons, and the occasional blue-ringed octopus
Hapalochlaena lunulata. Day diving on the outer wall is straightforward and
has good hard coral communities. Best season: June to October.

## 8. Miyako-jima

Miyako-jima and its adjacent islands (Irabu, Shimoji) have some of the clearest
water in Japan and a network of reef passages and underwater caves that includes
the famous Toriike Marine Lake on Ikema Island, where cave passages connect a
landlocked marine lake to the open sea. The outer reef walls have excellent
hard coral diversity and populations of Napoleon wrasse Cheilinus undulatus and
bumphead parrotfish. Best season: May to October.

## 9. Amami Oshima

Amami Oshima lies between Okinawa and Kyushu and has subtropical reef systems
that reach higher latitudes than most Japanese coral. The reefs here are
influenced by the warm Kuroshio Current and support branching and massive
hard coral species alongside the temperate fish communities that begin to appear
at these latitudes. Amami is also notable for a population of dugong Dugong
dugon that occasionally appears on seagrass beds behind the fringing reef.
Best season: April to October.

## 10. Ogasawara Islands (Bonin Islands), Tokyo

The Ogasawara Islands, administered by Tokyo despite being 1,000 kilometres to
the south, are a UNESCO Natural Heritage site accessible only by a 25-hour ferry
from Tokyo. The islands have no land-based connections to other islands and a
high proportion of endemic species, both terrestrial and marine. The reef systems
around Chichi-jima and Haha-jima are in outstanding condition with schooling
hammerheads, dolphins, and some of the best sea turtle diversity in Japan.
Best season: May to September.
