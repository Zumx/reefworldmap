The Maldives is a double chain of twenty-six atolls running 900 kilometres north
to south in the Indian Ocean, each atoll a ring of coral islands and reefs
enclosing a shallow lagoon. The characteristic dive sites are thilas — submerged
coral pinnacles that rise from the atoll floor to within a few metres of the
surface — and kandu, the passes through the atoll rim where tidal exchange
concentrates plankton and the pelagic species that feed on it. The Maldives
has been significantly affected by bleaching events, particularly in 1998, 2016,
and 2024, but its pelagic encounters and thilas remain among the Indian Ocean's
best. All sites are on the [interactive map](/map).

## 1. Banana Reef, North Male Atoll

Banana Reef is the oldest named dive site in the Maldives, shaped like a banana
and sitting in North Male Atoll just twenty minutes from the capital. Its coral
formations include caves, overhangs, and a fish life density that has been built
up over decades of dive tourism. Napoleon wrasse Cheilinus undulatus, grey reef
sharks Carcharhinus amblyrhynchos, and schooling jacks are regular. Boat access
from Male is straightforward. Best season: November to April.

## 2. Maaya Thila, Ari Atoll

Maaya Thila is consistently cited by experienced Maldivian dive guides as the
finest single thila in the country. At night, white-tip reef sharks Triaenodon
obesus hunt in coordinated groups around the pinnacle, and the night diving at
Maaya Thila is among the most dramatic in the Maldives. During the day, the thila
top has excellent hard coral and large resident Napoleon wrasse. Grey reef sharks
patrol the deeper sections. Best season: November to April.

## 3. Hammerhead Point, Rasdhoo Atoll

The outer wall of Rasdhoo Atoll drops sharply to open ocean, and scalloped
hammerheads Sphyrna lewini are present in the early morning at the wall's upper
section, typically between six-fifteen and seven a.m., before moving into
deeper water as daylight increases. The site requires a very early start from
nearby Rasdhoo Island but delivers among the most consistent hammerhead
encounters in the Maldives. Best season: November to April.

## 4. Lankan Manta Point, North Male Atoll

Lankan Manta Point (Lankan Finolhu) is a cleaning station in North Male Atoll
where reef mantas Mobula alfredi arrive in predictable numbers to be cleaned by
wrasse. The site is one of the most accessible manta cleaning stations in the
world — fifteen minutes from Male — and can host up to twenty mantas
simultaneously during peak feeding season. Divers hover above the reef and
watch the mantas circling in regular patterns. Best season: February to April.

## 5. Fish Head (Mushimasmingili Thila), Ari Atoll

Fish Head is famous for its aggregation of grey reef sharks Carcharhinus
amblyrhynchos, which can number over fifty individuals on a single dive, hovering
in the current off the thila. The pinnacle was previously a fish feeding site,
which may explain the unusual shark density; feeding has stopped but the sharks
remain habituated to diver presence. The thila also has good hard coral and
large populations of reef fish. Best season: November to April.

## 6. Vaadhoo Channel, South Male Atoll

Vaadhoo Channel is the main pass between South Male Atoll and Vaavu Atoll.
The strong tidal currents that run through it aggregate schooling fish,
eagle rays Aetobatus narinari, reef sharks, and — in season — whale sharks
Rhincodon typus. Channel diving in the Maldives requires drift technique and
current-reading ability; the Vaadhoo current can accelerate rapidly on the
tidal flood. Best season: November to February.

## 7. Hanifaru Bay, Baa Atoll (UNESCO Biosphere Reserve)

Hanifaru Bay in Baa Atoll is the world's largest known feeding aggregation of
manta rays. During the southwest monsoon, plankton concentrations in the shallow
bay attract reef mantas Mobula alfredi and whale sharks Rhincodon typus in numbers
that have reached five hundred mantas in a single day. The bay is a UNESCO
Biosphere Reserve site and entry is limited to a fixed number of snorkelers
per session (no scuba allowed inside the bay during feeding aggregations).
Best season: June to November.

## 8. Kandolhu House Reef, Ari Atoll

Kandolhu is a small resort island in Ari Atoll whose house reef is considered
exceptional for an inner-atoll fringing reef. The reef provides shore entry
and night diving access without a boat. Species recorded include pygmy seahorses
Hippocampus bargibanti on sea fans, various nudibranchs, leaf scorpionfish
Taenianotus triacanthus, and reef manta overflights. Best season: December
to April.

## 9. Cocoa Thila, North Male Atoll

Cocoa Thila, near Cocoa Island resort in South Male Atoll, is a multi-level
thila with excellent hard coral at the top and large sea fans and sponges on
the deeper slopes. Grey reef sharks are resident and Napoleon wrasse are
frequently seen. The site is close enough to Male to be included in short
stay itineraries. Best season: November to April.

## 10. Halaveli Wreck and Reef, Ari Atoll

Halaveli has both a purpose-sunk wreck at around twenty-eight metres — colonised
by soft coral and providing shelter for lionfish Pterois volitans, moray eels, and
reef fish — and an adjacent thila with exceptional hard coral cover on the upper
sections. The wreck diving combined with the natural reef makes Halaveli a site
that rewards multiple visits. Whitetip reef sharks rest in the wreck and under
coral overhangs on the thila. Best season: November to April.
